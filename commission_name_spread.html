Name and the Commission

Why autonomous instances must be named into accountability.
Thesis

Autonomous systems must be named, not numbered. A serial is a count; a name is an actor. The commission is the formal act of bringing a specific autonomous instance into the system of operational accountability — naming it, recognizing it as a discrete actor, binding it to the writ that authorizes it and the provenance that produced it. Anonymous instances are unaccountable instances. Type-level attribution is not actor-level accountability. The instance must have a name, or it cannot be answered for.

IThe Identity Problem

Current architectures treat autonomous platforms as instances of a type. Drone N of Type T. When N acts, the action is attributed to T (the platform class) with N as a serial. But T is a type, not an actor; types do not bear responsibility, types do not have histories, types are not answered for in legal or political review. The identity problem is that the move from type-based to instance-based accountability has not been made — the systems still treat their actors as countable interchangeables.

The commission frame insists this is the older shape of the question. Combatants have always been named. Vessels are named at launch. Units are commissioned with names that travel with them through their operational life and are formally retired at decommissioning. What modernity stripped from autonomous systems is not the underlying logic; it is the operational legibility. An autonomous platform without a bestowed name acts as an anonymous instrument, and anonymous instruments cannot be answered for — only their operators can, and only generically.

IIWhat the Commission Frame Predicts

Casualty attribution without named actor. When an autonomous platform is involved in a civilian casualty, current architectures attribute to the platform class — "an FW-MANIFEST/04 platform was responsible." The commission frame predicts that without named-instance accountability, the attribution is generic and so unactionable for legal review. The named sovereign cannot answer for an unnamed actor any more than a state can answer for an unnamed citizen.

Coalition operations under generic attribution. Multi-national autonomous fleets operate under coalition command. Without per-instance naming, partners cannot say this specific platform, with this specific commission, took this specific action under this writ. Generic attribution fragments coalition accountability — each sovereign attributes to the type but none to the actor, and the actor falls between them.

Lifecycle drift without ceremony. Autonomous platforms move from training to limited deployment to full operations to retirement. Without commission discipline, the state transitions are informal — software updates, configuration changes, scope expansions — and the platform's effective authority creeps without formal recognition. The commission frame predicts that named lifecycle states (commissioning, scope-expansion, decommissioning) prevent authority creep where informal transitions invite it.

IIIWhat the Commission Frame Recommends

  1. Every autonomous instance is named. Not a serial. Not a designator. A bestowed name, given by named authority, carried through the instance's operational life as part of every action it takes.
  2. The commission is ceremonial. A specific moment of formal naming, witnessed by named sovereign authority and named operational authority. The ceremony produces an artifact — the commission — that travels with the instance.
  3. The commission binds to writ and provenance. Every commissioned instance references its operating writ (the authority that warrants its actions) and its provenance manifest (the lineage that produced it). The name is the third pillar; writ and provenance are the other two.
  4. Decommissioning is the inverse ceremony. When the instance is retired, its name is formally retired with it. A retired name does not return; a new instance requires a new commission. Names are not recycled. The retirement is itself an act of sovereignty.

IVCoda

Naming brings into accountability. Without commission, the autonomous system is a type acting through interchangeable instances — and types cannot be answered for. With commission, the system is a named actor whose actions are attributable, whose history is traceable, and whose retirement is itself an act of recognized sovereignty. The writ named who could authorize; the provenance named what produced the actor; the commission names the actor itself. Three names, one artifact: the autonomous instance fully legible to the system of accountability that warrants it.

IRON RULESR.01—R.05
  1. Names are bestowed, not assigned. A bestowed name is given by named authority through a recognized ceremony; an assigned designation is generated by the system. The commission requires a name, not a designation.
  2. Identity is anchored to writ and provenance. Every commission references its operating writ (the authority warranting its actions) and its provenance manifest (the lineage that produced it). Orphan commissions are forbidden.
  3. The blazon is the mark. The heraldic identity of the named instance is rendered in Sigil Mauve at the center of the commission. The blazon is the recognition itself in visible form.
  4. Each attestation is one recognition moment. Sovereign recognition, naming officer's bestowal, operational authority's acceptance. Each is a separate seal in Sigil Mauve. Decorative use of Sigil Mauve is forbidden.
  5. Decommissioning is the inverse ceremony. Retirement of the instance retires its name with it. Retired names do not return; new instances require new commissions. Names are not recycled across instances.
