Memory and the Provenance

Why autonomous action needs legible lineage.
Thesis

Autonomous systems do not arrive de novo. Each system is the latest delegate of a stack of upstream choices — training corpora curated by named persons, weights trained against named criteria, sensors calibrated against named standards, code maintained by named contributors. When the system acts, it acts on the inheritance of every upstream decision that produced it. Without legible provenance, that inheritance is invisible to the inheritor; the system's action cannot be audited beyond its immediate cause. The provenance frame insists every autonomous system carry, as part of the operational artifact, a tractable record of what made it possible.

IThe Provenance Problem

Current architectures of autonomous defense systems treat upstream contributions as supply-chain risk rather than as operational dependencies. The model card lives in a separate registry; the training data manifest lives in a separate archive; the platform's hardware bill of materials lives in a separate procurement record. The system itself does not carry these records — they are not part of the artifact that acts. When something goes wrong, the records are reassembled by archaeologists from artifacts that were never designed for forward inspection.

The provenance frame insists this is the older shape of the problem. Sovereignty has always operated through named lineage — the writ that traces back to the king, the deed that traces back to the grantor, the title that traces back to the patent. What modernity stripped is not the underlying logic; it is the operational legibility. An autonomous system that acts without carrying its provenance acts in the same posture as a contract without consideration — capable of execution, but missing the inheritance that would make the execution lawful or reviewable.

IIWhat the Provenance Frame Predicts

Training-data provenance failure. When a model is deployed without its training data manifest, you cannot determine what regularities it learned, what biases it inherited, what edge cases were undersampled. After-action review reconstructs the dataset by inference. The provenance frame predicts this reconstruction is unauditable — any post-hoc analysis recovers a plausible training corpus, not the actual one.

Weight provenance failure. When deployed weights cannot be traced to a specific training run with named inputs and seed, the system's behavior cannot be reproduced for audit. Two tensors that appear identical may have descended from different runs with different biases. The provenance frame predicts that without per-tensor lineage, autonomous systems are reproducibility-fragile and so unauditable in any but the loosest sense.

Institutional memory failure. When a successor autonomous system inherits from a predecessor but the inheritance is informal — commit messages, tribal knowledge, retained-employee memory — the successor cannot inspect what the predecessor learned. The provenance frame predicts successor systems repeat predecessor mistakes, not because the mistakes are intrinsic to the doctrine but because the predecessor's learning is invisible to the inheritor.

IIIWhat the Provenance Frame Recommends

  1. Every system carries its manifest. Not in registries accessible on request. Not in archives available for audit. In the artifact itself, inspectable at the moment of action.
  2. Provenance is named, not implicit. Specific persons, specific training runs, specific vendors, specific dates. "Trained on the standard corpus" is not provenance; "Trained on Corpus FW-2024-Q3, curated by [Named], dated 2024-08-22, hash a3f9c2…" is.
  3. Provenance is inheritable. A successor system must be able to read and verify the predecessor's manifest before assuming inheritance. The manifest is designed for forward inspection — what the next generation needs — not just backward audit.

IVCoda

The discipline insists that what produces the system is part of what the system shows. Autonomous action without provenance is action without a past, and the operator inheriting the system cannot know what the system itself inherited. The named sovereign (per Folio №05 WRIT) and the named provenance (this folio) together produce a system that is auditable both for its authority and for its origins. One without the other leaves the system structurally incomplete: authority cannot examine what it is authorizing, or provenance cannot point to who warrants the action. Both are required for the artifact to be inspectable as a whole.

IRON RULESR.01—R.05
  1. Provenance is named. Each generation in the manifest names a specific custodian and a specific artifact. Implicit, generic, or anonymous custody is not provenance.
  2. Each generation is hashed. Every artifact in the chain carries a verification hash. Generations without hashes cannot be verified and so cannot anchor inheritance.
  3. The chain is unbroken. Gaps in the lineage invalidate the manifest. If a generation cannot be named or hashed, the manifest is incomplete and the system inherits from an incomplete source.
  4. The seal is the mark. Compact Green appears only at custody transitions. Each seal marks one named custodian's attestation that they received from the prior generation and pass to the next. Decorative use is forbidden.
  5. The manifest is forward-inspectable. A successor system must be able to read and verify the entire chain before assuming inheritance. The manifest is designed for what the next generation needs, not just for retrospective audit.
